Research Overview

I investigate how reef systems and sea level have changed through time, combining U–Th geochronology, carbonate geochemistry, and coral proxy records with transparent, Python-based analysis. My work focuses on extracting seasonal to multi-decadal variability (e.g., ENSO) from coral archives and on improving chronologies by screening and dating meteoric calcite phases in altered fossil corals.

I pair careful field sampling with laboratory tools (LA-ICP-MS, MC-ICP-MS, micro-CT/3D, SEM), emphasizing rigorous QA/QC for low-level trace elements and REEs. The goal is decision-relevant reconstructions of ocean–climate history built on reproducible methods and open, well-documented data reduction.

Research Focus

Coral Paleoclimate Archives

Seasonal to multi-decadal reconstructions from coral trace elements and isotopes (Mg/Ca, Li/Mg, δ13C). Time-series pipelines support seasonality extraction, anomaly detection, and event characterization linked to ENSO and hydroclimate.

U–Th Geochronology

High-precision dating of carbonates, including meteoric calcite phases in altered fossil corals, to refine Holocene sea-level histories. Work includes micro-sampling strategies, spike calibration, and open-system checks to minimize age bias and reversals.

Method Development

Instrument optimization, standards and QC design, and REE/trace-element workflows at ultra-low levels. I build reproducible, Python-based data-reduction notebooks and validation tests to ensure robust, comparable results.

Current Projects

Sea Level from Meteoric Calcite

U–Th dating of meteoric calcite in highly altered fossil corals. Trace-element screening, micro-sampling, and open-system checks improve chronologies and reduce uncertainty in Holocene sea-level reconstructions.

Mid-Holocene Coral Paleoclimate (Heron Reef)

Using fossil corals from Heron Reef, we built two ~60-year snapshots at ~6.9 and 5.2 ka. Band-by-band laser measurements of Mg, Sr, Mn, and rare-earth elements track temperature, water quality, and river-plume influence. The records test how ENSO (El Niño–La Niña) and the Indo-Australian Summer Monsoon behaved in the mid-Holocene. A new calibration step strengthens faint signals, revealing links between climate swings and coastal water quality on the Great Barrier Reef long before instruments.
